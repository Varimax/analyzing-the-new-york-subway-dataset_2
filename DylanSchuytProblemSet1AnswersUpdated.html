Section 1. Statistical Test

1.1 Which statistical test did you use to analyse the NYC subway data? Did you use a one-tail or a two-tail P value? What is the null hypothesis? What is your p-critical value?

I used a two-tail test on the data to test my hypothesis that “Ridership was not affected by the weather being rainy”. Once completed I had a P score of 0.024999, which is way below the P critical threshold of 0.10 and therefore we can reject the null hypothesis that ridership would not be affected by rain and have to accept that ridership is affected by whether or not it is raining.

1.2 Why is this statistical test applicable to the dataset? In particular, consider the assumptions that the test is making about the distribution of ridership in the two samples.

The dataset was not normally distributed, as per the results of Problem Set 3 question 1, and so we could not run a T-test on the data

1.3 What results did you get from this statistical test? These should include the following numerical values: p-values, as well as the means for each of the two samples under test.

  • We can reject the null hypothesis because ridership does change depending on the weather.
  • P  < 0.10
  • Mean With Rain = 1105.45
  • Mean Without Rain = 1090.28

1.4 What is the significance and interpretation of these results?

We can say that with a confidence interval of 0.10 that there will be more riders on the train when it is raining

I used the following input variables for my model: Rain, precipi, hour, meantempi

I did use the UNIT dummy variable, but this does mean that we would have to be careful not to use other dummy variables if we choose to use OLS as that could cause multicollinearity. 2.3 Why did you select these features in your model? We are looking for specific reasons that lead you to believe that the selected features will contribute to the predictive power of your model.

I decided to use rain, precipitation, time and the average temperature as intuitively the weather causes me to sooner take the subway. Also after having tested & rejecting the initial null hypothesis, “Ridership was not affected by the weather being rainy”, I looked for variable that I knew were likely related to rainy weather. Resulting in a significant jump in R2  indicating that the sample agrees with me!

Yes they do.

 

4.2 What analyses lead you to this conclusion? You should use results from both your statistical tests and your linear regression to support your analysis.

After checking the distribution of the data it was determined that there is a variation of ridership depending on the weather. 5.1 Please discuss potential shortcomings of the methods of your analysis, including: Dataset, Linear regression model, Statistical test.

There is a long list of potential shortcomings with this particular analysis. All of the weather data for instance, seems to have a high likelihood of causing multicollinearity in the model and thereby generating a false positive. Additionally we haven’t performed any analysis on the residuals, which might hold additional information towards the validity of our model.

Using a linear regression doesn’t seem to be the optimal choice when using binary variables (rain = 0/1) and would probably be better to use a binary logistic regression. Also, 46% explanatory power is sufficient for this particular exercise, but in a more real life scenario we would prefer to be at 80+%.
